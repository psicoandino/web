# ARIES
ORIGIN:  
* ****Names/Tradition:**** **Lu.hung.ga** (Sumerian/Mesopotamian).  
* ****Figure/Entity:**** Represented originally as the "Hired Farm Labourer" or "Hired Man". In the later Greco-Roman tradition, it is represented as the Ram.  
DOMAIN:  
* Aries is identified as the marker for the "first age of man" or babyhood in some schemas, though specifically it is the constellation/sign governing the head and face in the "zodiac man" anatomical mapping. It is associated with headstrong, fierce, and "bestial" characteristics in mundane geographic assignments.  
* ****Is there consensus between ≥2 traditions with no known historical contact?**** No; the sources loaded in this notebook document a clear line of transmission from the Mesopotamian "Hired Man" to the Greco-Roman and Western European medical traditions.  
HISTORICAL_VOICES:  
* ****Mesopotamian**** | 7th century BC | Known as the "Hired Farm Labourer" (**Lú.Hun.Gá**); in early astronomical records like the **MUL.APIN**, it was the final deity/constellation in the path of the Moon, which at that time began the sequence with Taurus. | "Celestial Symbols"; Nick Kollerstrom, **The Star Zodiac of Antiquity**, 1997 | secondary synthesis.  
* ****Greco-Roman**** | 2nd century AD | Defined as a "tropical sign" whose beginning is fixed to the mathematically calculated vernal equinox; it is categorized as masculine, diurnal, and the "house" of Mars. It is assigned to govern Britain, making its inhabitants headstrong and fierce. | Claudius Ptolemy, **Tetrabiblos** (Hübner ed.) | primary.  
* ****English Star Medicine**** | 17th century | Rulership over the head and face is used to determine remedies; for example, Blessed Thistle (a herb of Mars) is used to treat vertigo and head pains because "Aries is in the house of Mars" and governs the head. | Graeme Tobyn, **The English Physitian and the Star Medicine**, 2022 | secondary synthesis.  
IDENTIFIED_CONTRADICTIONS:  
* ****Sequence Position:**** Early Mesopotamian records place Aries at the end of the sequence of constellations (which began with Taurus/Pleiades), whereas the Greco-Roman tradition established it as the definitive beginning of the zodiac.  
* ****Iconography:**** There is a total shift in representation from the Mesopotamian "Hired Worker" to the Greco-Roman "Ram".  
  
# TAURUS  
ORIGIN:  
* **Names/Tradition:** *Gu.an.na* (Mesopotamian/Sumerian). The specific asterism comprising the head was known as "The Jaw of the Bull" (*Is.li.e*).  
* **Figure/Entity:** Represents the "Bull of Heaven," a mythological entity from the Epic of Gilgamesh. In the Greco-Roman tradition, it remains represented as the Bull.  
DOMAIN:  
* Taurus symbolizes biological stability and the established height of the spring season; in anatomical mapping (melothesia), it specifically governs the neck and throat. It is categorized as a feminine, nocturnal, and "solid" sign that serves as the "house" of the planet Venus.  
* **Is there consensus between ≥2 traditions with no known historical contact?** No; the loaded sources document a clear line of cultural transmission and iconographic evolution from Mesopotamian foundations to Greco-Roman and Western European traditions.  
HISTORICAL_VOICES:  
* **Mesopotamian** | 7th century BC | Documented in the *MUL.APIN* as the "Jaw of the Bull" (*Is.li.e*); it was the first constellation in the path of the Moon, beginning the sequence of eighteen star-deities that defined the ecliptic sectors | "Celestial Symbols"; Nick Kollerstrom, *The Star Zodiac of Antiquity*, 1997 | secondary synthesis.  
* **Greco-Roman** | 2nd century AD | Defined as a "solid" sign where the Sun's latitudinal progress is established; it is of a temperate, moistening nature and serves as the domicile of Venus. Ptolemy specifies that its effects vary by section, particularly near the Pleiades and Hyades clusters | Claudius Ptolemy, *Tetrabiblos* (Hübner ed.) | primary.  
* **English Star Medicine** | 17th century | Assigned to the neck and throat in the "zodiac man" schema. Used to determine remedies through sympathy or antipathy; for instance, the herb lovage (ruled by the Sun in Taurus) was used to treat throat maladies caused by Saturn | Graeme Tobyn, *The English Physitian and the Star Medicine*, 2022 | secondary synthesis.  
IDENTIFIED_CONTRADICTIONS:  
* **Zodiac Sequence:** Early Mesopotamian records (*MUL.APIN*) place Taurus at the beginning of the sequence of constellations, whereas the Greco-Roman tradition and later Western systems established Aries as the definitive start of the zodiac.  
* **Iconography:** While both traditions use the bull, the Mesopotamian "Bull of Heaven" was visually distinct from the later Greco-Roman representation, though both shared the Hyades as the figure's head.  

# GEMINI  
ORIGIN:  
* **Names/Tradition:** Mesopotamian (Sumerian/Babylonian).  
* **Figure/Entity:** Represents the "Great Twins" of the underworld, identified specifically as the deities *Lugalirra* and *Meslamtaea*. In the later Greco-Roman tradition, these were unifed and transformed into the celestial Twins.  
DOMAIN:  
* Gemini is characterized as a "bi-corporeal" or double sign that shares the shifting weather properties of the end of one season and the beginning of the next; anatomically, it governs the arms and shoulders. It is categorized as the "house" of the planet Mercury.  
* **Is there consensus between ≥2 traditions with no known historical contact?** No; the sources document a direct line of cultural transmission and rationalization from Mesopotamian asterisms to the Greco-Roman zodiac and subsequent Western medical astrology.  
HISTORICAL_VOICES:  
* **Mesopotamian** | 7th century BC | Recorded in the *MUL.APIN* and other cuneiform sources as the "Great Twins" (*MAŠ.TAB.BA.GAL.GAL*), personifying the underworld gods *Lugalirra* and *Meslamtaea*. | "Celestial Symbols"; Nick Kollerstrom, *The Star Zodiac of Antiquity*, 1997 | secondary synthesis.  
* **Greco-Roman** | 2nd century AD | Classified as a "bi-corporeal" sign because it shares the natural properties of two states of weather. It is the domicile of Mercury and part of the triplicity governing the North-East quarter of the inhabited world (Scythia). | Claudius Ptolemy, *Tetrabiblos* (Hübner ed.) | primary.  
* **English Star Medicine** | 17th century | Assigned to the arms and shoulders in the "zodiac man" schema. Practitioners used its association with Mercury to determine sympathetic or antipathetic treatments for specific ailments. | Graeme Tobyn, *The English Physitian and the Star Medicine*, 2022 | secondary synthesis.  
IDENTIFIED_CONTRADICTIONS:  
* **Mythological Nature:** In the early Mesopotamian tradition, the constellation is associated with specific deities of the underworld (*Lugalirra* and *Meslamtaea*), whereas the later Greco-Roman and Western traditions focus on its "bi-corporeal" nature and its link to the planetary characteristics of Mercury.

# VIRGO  
ORIGIN:  
* **Names/Tradition:** *Absin* (Sumerian/Mesopotamian). In Greek, *Parthenos*.  
* **Figure/Entity:** Originally represented as "The Furrow" or "The Furrow of Seed," personifying the goddess Shala of fertility. In later Greco-Roman and Egyptian traditions, it was transformed into the figure of a Virgin or an Isis-type deity holding a sheaf of corn (identified with the star Spica).  
DOMAIN:  
* Virgo symbolizes agricultural fertility and the harvest; in anatomical mapping (melothesia), it governs the bowels and the belly. It is categorized as a feminine, nocturnal, and "bi-corporeal" sign that serves as the planetary domicile (house) and exaltation of Mercury.  
* **Is there consensus between ≥2 traditions with no known historical contact?** No; the loaded sources document a direct line of cultural and iconographic transmission from the Mesopotamian "Furrow" to the Greco-Roman "Virgin" and subsequent Western medical traditions.  
HISTORICAL_VOICES:  
* **Mesopotamian** | 7th century BC | Known as "The Furrow" (*Absin*); it was one of the deities "standing on the path of the moon" through whose sectors the Moon passed every month. | "Celestial Symbols"; Nick Kollerstrom, *The Star Zodiac of Antiquity*, 1997 | secondary synthesis.  
* **Egyptian** | c. 30 BC | Represented in the Dendera zodiac as an upright Isis-type figure holding a sheaf of corn. | Nick Kollerstrom, *The Star Zodiac of Antiquity*, 1997 | secondary synthesis.  
* **Greco-Roman** | 2nd century AD | Classified as a "bi-corporeal" sign sharing properties of two states of weather; it is defined as the domicile of Mercury and part of the triplicity governing the south-eastern quarter of the inhabited world (Greater Asia). | Claudius Ptolemy, *Tetrabiblos* (Hübner ed.) | primary.  
* **English Star Medicine** | 17th century | Mapped to the bowels and belly in the "zodiac man" anatomical schema. Practitioners used its association with Mercury to determine sympathetic or antipathetic treatments for ailments located in the midsection of the body. | Graeme Tobyn, *The English Physitian and the Star Medicine*, 2022 | secondary synthesis.  
IDENTIFIED_CONTRADICTIONS:  
* **Iconographic Posture:** Early representations (Babylonian fragments and the Dendera zodiac) depict Virgo as an *upright* figure, whereas Ptolemy’s later description presents a very large constellation figure (spanning 46 degrees) with the Virgin "laid out on her back."  
* **Position of Spica:** In the definition of the sidereal star-zodiac, Spica was a boundary star (positioned at 29° or 30° Virgo), while in Ptolemy's constellational description, Spica is located in the middle of the figure.  
* **Mythological Identity:** There is a total shift from the Mesopotamian identification as a literal agricultural "Furrow" to the Greco-Roman personification as a "Virgin."

# SCORPIUS  
ORIGIN:  
* **Names/Tradition:** *Girtab* (Mesopotamian/Sumerian); *Chelae* (Greek), originally referring to the "Claws" of the Scorpion that later became Libra.  
* **Figure/Entity:** Represents "The Scorpion". In Mesopotamian tradition, it personified the goddess Ishhara; in Ancient Egypt, its brightest star (Antares) represented the scorpion goddess Serket and was associated with Isis.  
DOMAIN:  
* Scorpius symbolizes established seasonal stability (as a "solid" sign) and is anatomically mapped to the "secret members" or reproductive organs in medical astrology.  
* **Is there consensus between ≥2 traditions with no known historical contact?** Yes; the Mesopotamian, Egyptian, Hindu, and Aztec traditions independently identified this star region with the figure of a scorpion or associated it with scorpion-deities.  
HISTORICAL_VOICES:

* **Ancient Egyptian** | Undated | The star Antares represented the goddess Serket and served as a celestial signifier in pyramidal ceremonies associated with Isis. | Ioannis Liritzis et al., *Asclepius and Apollo Cult Archaeoastronomy*, 2017 | secondary synthesis.  
* **Greco-Roman** | 2nd century AD | Defined as a "solid" sign and part of the triplicity (including Cancer and Pisces) that governs the south-western quarter of the inhabited world, specifically Northwest Africa (Ancient Libya). | Claudius Ptolemy, *Tetrabiblos* (Wikipedia summary) | primary.  
* **Greco-Roman** | 2nd century AD | Ptolemy assigns the stars in the "body of Scorpio" a temperature "like that of Mars," while those in the claws are like Jupiter and Mercury. | Nick Kollerstrom, *The Star Zodiac of Antiquity*, 1997 (citing *Tetrabiblos* I.9) | secondary synthesis.

* **English Star Medicine** | 17th century | Mapped to the "secret members" (reproductive organs) in the "zodiac man" anatomical schema used for determining herbal remedies. | Graeme Tobyn, *The English Physitian and the Star Medicine*, 2022 | secondary synthesis.

* **Constellation Boundary:** Early Mesopotamian and Greek traditions viewed the Scorpion as a much larger entity that included the "Claws" (modern Libra), whereas the later Greco-Roman mathematical tradition formally separated them into two distinct signs.  

 # OPHIOUCHUS 
ORIGIN:
- **Names/Tradition:** *Ophioukhos* (Greek), meaning "serpent-bearer". It was formerly referred to as *Serpentarius* in Latin.
- **Figure/Entity:** Represents the Greek healer-deity Asclepius (Aesculapius in Roman tradition). According to the myth, he was the son of Apollo and the mortal Coronis, educated in medicine by the centaur Chiron. After mastering the ability to restore life to the dead, he was killed by Jupiter with a thunderbolt at the request of Pluto, who feared the depopulation of the underworld. Jupiter then placed him in the sky holding the two segments of the constellation Serpens (*Serpens Caput* and *Serpens Cauda*).

DOMAIN:
- Ophiuchus symbolizes the abstraction of human medical and surgical proficiency to a divine and metaphysical level, personifying regeneration, the mastery of curative roots, and healing light.
- **Is there consensus between ≥2 traditions with no known historical contact?** No; while both Greek and Egyptian traditions associate the region with healer-deities (Asclepius and Imhotep, respectively), these cultures were in active contact during the documented periods.

HISTORICAL_VOICES:
- **Greco-Roman** | 2nd century AD | Classified as one of the 48 constellations; identified as the "Serpent-bearer" representing Asclepius. | Claudius Ptolemy, *Almagest* / *Tetrabiblos* | primary.
- **Greco-Roman** | 1st century AD | Defined as an influential "extra-zodiacal" constellation (*paranatellonta*) whose rising and setting affects human character. | Manilius, *Astronomica* | primary.
- **Ancient Egyptian** | Undated | Associated with the healer-deity Imhotep and utilized as a celestial signifier in ceremonies. | Ioannis Liritzis et al., *Asclepius and Apollo Cult Archaeoastronomy*, 2017 | secondary synthesis.
- **Phrygian** | ca. 100 AD | Linked to the healing nature of the sanctuary of Apollo Lairbenos, potentially associated with the local lunar deity Mên. | Ioannis Liritzis et al., *Asclepius and Apollo Cult Archaeoastronomy*, 2017 | secondary synthesis.
- **Greek Archaeoastronomy** | 7th century BC – 2nd century AD | Sanctuaries dedicated to the healing cults of Apollo and Asclepius (such as Epidaurus and Messene) were intentionally aligned to the heliacal rising or setting of Ophiuchus to ensure ritual success. | Ioannis Liritzis et al., *Asclepius and Apollo Cult Archaeoastronomy*, 2017 | secondary synthesis.
- **IAU** | 1930 | Formally defined with scientific boundaries that establish it as the thirteenth constellation through which the Sun crosses the ecliptic annually. | Eugène Joseph Delporte, *Délimitation Scientifique des Constellations* | primary (reported in secondary synthesis).

IDENTIFIED_CONTRADICTIONS:
- **Zodiacal Status:** Classical authorities (Ptolemy, Manilius, Hübner) categorize Ophiuchus as an "extra-zodiacal" or *paranatellonta* constellation that exists outside the twelve-sign mathematical grid. This contradicts the 1930 IAU scientific delimitation which formally integrates it as a thirteenth constellation of the ecliptic path.
- **Iconography:** While standard Greek sources depict Ophiuchus as a man holding a serpent, some archaeoastronomical contexts in Asia Minor associate the healing nature of the site with lunar or solar attributes rather than the serpent-bearer figure specifically.

*Note: The loaded sources do not provide a documented Mesopotamian or Sumerian name for Ophiuchus equivalent to the names provided for the twelve standard zodiacal constellations.*

DOMAIN:  
* Pisces symbolizes seasonal transition as a "bi-corporeal" sign and is associated with maritime affairs, the sea, and floods; anatomically, it is mapped specifically to the feet.

HISTORICAL_VOICES:  
* **Mesopotamian** | 7th century BC | Comprised of two separate asterisms, the "Great Swallow" and the "Lady of the Heavens," which were identified as deities "standing on the path of the moon" through whose sectors the Moon passed monthly | "Celestial Symbols"; Nick Kollerstrom, *The Star Zodiac of Antiquity*, 1997 | secondary synthesis.  
* **Mesopotamian** | 6th century BC | As a result of the reorganization of the zodiac into twelve equal signs, several independent constellations in this stellar region were fused into the single sign of Pisces | Nick Kollerstrom, *The Star Zodiac of Antiquity*, 1997 | secondary synthesis.  
* **Greco-Roman** | 2nd century AD | Classified as a "bi-corporeal" sign sharing the properties of two states of weather (the end of one season and beginning of the next); it is defined as feminine, nocturnal, and the planetary domicile (house) of Jupiter | Claudius Ptolemy, *Tetrabiblos* | primary source.  
* **Greco-Roman** | 2nd century AD | Part of the 'Cancer triplicity' (including Cancer and Scorpio) which governs the south-western quarter of the inhabited world, specifically Northwest Africa (Ancient Libya) | Claudius Ptolemy, *Tetrabiblos* | primary source.  
* **Early Christian** | 2nd century AD | The fish symbol was adopted in Alexandria to represent the new religion, coinciding with the transition of the Vernal Point into the sidereal sign of Pisces due to precession | Nick Kollerstrom, *The Star Zodiac of Antiquity*, 1997 | secondary synthesis.  
* **English Star Medicine** | 17th century | Mapped specifically to the feet in the "zodiac man" anatomical schema; the sign's ruler, Jupiter, is utilized to determine treatments and is considered an essential enemy of the planet Mercury | Graeme Tobyn, *The English Physitian and the Star Medicine*, 2022 | secondary synthesis.  
IDENTIFIED_CONTRADICTIONS:  
* **Iconographic Evolution:** There is a documented historical shift from the Mesopotamian tradition of two distinct and biologically different figures (a swallow and a goddess) to the unified Greco-Roman representation of two "Fishes".  
*Note: There is a gap in the loaded sources regarding the identification or symbolism of Pisces in Mesoamerican or Pre-Neolithic traditions, which are documented for other ecliptic constellations.*
