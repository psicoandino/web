# SUN
DOMAIN:
- The Sun governs the primary seasonal and daily cycles of the Earth, acting as the fundamental source of heat, light, and "diurnal" vitality. It illuminates the transition into early manhood (ages 22–40) and is mapped anatomically to the heart and the faculty of eyesight.
- **Is there consensus between ≥2 traditions with no known historical contact?** Yes; the Greco-Roman, Mesoamerican, and Inca traditions independently utilized the Sun's extreme horizon positions (solstices and equinoxes) to orient monumental architecture and synchronize sacred ritual calendars.

HISTORICAL_VOICES:
- **Mesopotamian** | 7th–5th century BC | A twelve-fold "solar logic" was stamped upon hitherto irregular constellations to create equal 30-degree signs; the Sun's movement of approximately one degree per day defined the schematic year. | Nick Kollerstrom, *The Star Zodiac of Antiquity*, 1997 | secondary synthesis.
- **Greco-Roman** | 2nd century AD | The primary influence on Earth's environment; it defines the "tropical signs" (Cancer and Capricorn) where it reverses its latitudinal progress. In nativities, it governs the fourth age of man (early manhood), characterized by ambition and responsibility, and signifies the husband in a woman's chart. | Claudius Ptolemy, *Tetrabiblos* (Hübner ed.) | primary.
- **Maya** | Classic and Postclassic | Civic and ceremonial buildings were systematically aligned to sunrises and sunsets on particular dates; the "night Sun" represented a liminal, nocturnal aspect of the luminary linked to the HERO twin Xbalanqué and fertility. | Ivan Šprajc, *Lunar Alignments in Mesoamerican Architecture*, 2016 | secondary synthesis.
- **Inca** | Tahuantinsuyu period | State-sanctioned observations of the June solstice were conducted by both elite and non-elite populations, particularly at the Island of the Sun. | Clive Ruggles (Ed.), *Handbook of Archaeoastronomy and Ethnoastronomy*, 2015 | secondary synthesis.
- **English Star Medicine** | 17th century | A hot and dry body that governs the heart and the eyes; its herbs (such as lovage or eyebright) are used through sympathy to strengthen sight or treat ailments caused by the "cold" planet Saturn. | Graeme Tobyn, *The English Physitian and the Star Medicine*, 2022 | secondary synthesis.

IDENTIFIED_CONTRADICTIONS:
- None identified; the sources demonstrate high cross-cultural consistency regarding the Sun's role as a marker for time, seasons, and architectural orientation.

# MOON
DOMAIN:
- The Moon governs biological growth, the physical nourishment of the body (specifically in infancy), and the synchronization of natural water cycles including tides, rainfall, and agricultural fertility. It also illuminates the sensory, irrational, and subconscious aspects of the human soul.
- **Is there consensus between ≥2 traditions with no known historical contact?** Yes; the Maya and Greco-Roman traditions independently associate the Moon with water/moisture cycles, biological fertility, and the regulation of agricultural growth.

HISTORICAL_VOICES:
- **Mesopotamian** | 7th century BC | The "path of the moon" defines the sectors of 17 or 18 star deities through which the body passes monthly, establishing the foundations of the zodiac. | Nick Kollerstrom, *The Star Zodiac of Antiquity*, 1997 | secondary synthesis.
- **Greco-Roman** | 2nd century AD | A feminine luminary of a moistening nature that rules the "first age of man" (babyhood), characterized by growth and changeability; it also governs the sensory soul and marital indications for men. | Claudius Ptolemy, *Tetrabiblos* (as synthesized in "Tetrabiblos - Wikipedia") | primary.
- **Maya** | Classic to Postclassic | Represented by the goddess Ixchel; governs pregnancy, childbirth, medicine, and weaving, with its 18.6-year standstill cycles determining the alignment of coastal pilgrimage sanctuaries. | Ivan Šprajc, *Lunar Alignments in Mesoamerican Architecture*, 2016 | secondary synthesis.
- **Inca** | Tahuantinsuyu period | Systematic observations of the Moon were conducted and integrated into the state’s complex calendar system. | Clive Ruggles (Ed.), *Handbook of Archaeoastronomy and Ethnoastronomy*, 2015 | secondary synthesis.
- **English Star Medicine** | 17th century | A cold and moist body that governs the brain and phlegmatic humours; its herbs (such as wild lettuce) are used through sympathy to balance bodily heat. | Graeme Tobyn, *The English Physitian and the Star Medicine*, 2022 | secondary synthesis.
- **Phrygian** | c. 100 AD | The lunar deity Mên was worshipped as a healing figure and associated with the curative rites of Apollo and Asclepius. | Ioannis Liritzis et al., *Asclepius and Apollo Cult Archaeoastronomy*, 2017 | secondary synthesis.

IDENTIFIED_CONTRADICTIONS:
- In the Mesoamerican context, the Moon is explicitly documented as a ruler of military cycles and warfare ritual, a domain not identified in the Greco-Roman or English medical traditions, which focus instead on domesticity, nourishment, and temperament.
- Academic debate exists within Maya studies regarding whether the goddess Ixchel is strictly lunar (Goddess I) or a deity of water and weaving (Goddess O), though secondary synthesis suggests these figures represent different phases or aspects of the same celestial body.

# MERCURY
DOMAIN:
- Mercury illuminates the development of intelligence, conscious reasoning, articulation, and commerce; it is characterized as the governor of the "second age of man" (childhood, ages 4–13) and the anatomical ruler of the brain and physical/mental dexterity.
- **Is there consensus between ≥2 traditions with no known historical contact?** No; the sources document a transmission of Mercury’s attributes from Mesopotamian foundations to Greco-Roman and subsequent Western European medical traditions.

HISTORICAL_VOICES:
- **Mesopotamian** | 7th century BC | Included as a planetary deity in the *MUL.APIN* astronomical compendium. | Hunger & Pingree, *MUL.APIN: An Astronomical Compendium in Cuneiform*, 1989 | secondary synthesis.
- **Greco-Roman** | Classical Period | Known as *Stilbon* ("The Gleaming"); assigned to the god Hermes (Mercury) as the fastest planet, serving as the messenger of the gods and protector of travelers. | "Celestial Symbols: A Critical Guide to Cosmographic Bibliography" | secondary synthesis.
- **Greco-Roman** | 2nd century AD | Governs the second age of life (childhood) and the rational faculty of the soul; its condition determines articulated speech and the capacity for conscious reasoning. | Claudius Ptolemy, *Tetrabiblos* | primary.
- **English Star Medicine** | 17th century | A planet of a cold and dry nature that governs the brain and lungs; it is the ruler of the signs Gemini and Virgo. | Graeme Tobyn, *The English Physitian and the Star Medicine*, 2022 | secondary synthesis.
- **English Star Medicine** | 17th century | Identified as an "essential enemy" of the planets Jupiter and Venus, but a friend to Saturn. | Nicholas Culpeper, *Semeiotica Uranica*, 1651 (reported in Graeme Tobyn, 2022) | secondary synthesis.

IDENTIFIED_CONTRADICTIONS:
- None identified; the traditions documented in these sources maintain consistent associations between Mercury and the qualities of speed, intelligence, and the governance of the brain.

# VENUS
DOMAIN:
Venus illuminates the spheres of biological fertility, moisture regulation, and the development of human passion and sexuality. It is mapped anatomically to the kidneys and the reproductive organs, and it governs the "third age of man," defined as youth. 
**Is there consensus between ≥2 traditions with no known historical contact?** Yes; the Mesoamerican and Greco-Roman traditions independently associate Venus with the regulation of water/rain cycles and biological fertility.

HISTORICAL_VOICES:
- **Mesopotamian** | 7th century BC | Included as a planetary deity in the *MUL.APIN* astronomical compendium, where it served as one of the "errant" gods whose movements were tracked for divination. | Hunger & Pingree, *MUL.APIN: An Astronomical Compendium in Cuneiform*, 1989 | secondary synthesis.
- **Greco-Roman** | Classical Period | Known as *Phosphoros* ("The Light-bringer") as the morning and evening star; it was consecrated to the goddess Aphrodite (Venus) as the brightest and most beautiful of the visible errant bodies. | "Celestial Symbols: A Critical Guide to Cosmographic Bibliography" | secondary synthesis.
- **Greco-Roman** | 2nd century AD | Governs the third age of man (youth, ages 14–21), characterized by burning passion, sexuality, and guile. It is categorized as a feminine, nocturnal planet with a temperate, moistening nature and a yellow celestial color used for judging eclipse omens. | Claudius Ptolemy, *Tetrabiblos* (as synthesized in "Tetrabiblos - Wikipedia") | primary.
- **Mesoamerican (Maya/Olmec)** | Classic and Postclassic | Temples and palaces were systematically aligned to its horizon extremes to signal the start of the rainy season. It is linked to the "night Sun," maize fertility, and "ritual war". | Ivan Šprajc, *Venus in Mesoamerica*, 2018 (cited in "Celestial Symbols") | secondary synthesis.
- **English Star Medicine** | 17th century | A feminine and temperate planet that rules the kidneys and the sign of Libra. Its herbs, such as kidneywort or plums, are used to strengthen the kidneys or reproductive health through sympathy. | Graeme Tobyn, *The English Physitian and the Star Medicine*, 2022 | secondary synthesis.
- **English Star Medicine** | 17th century | Classified as an "essential enemy" of the Sun, Mars, and Mercury, but recognized as a friend to Jupiter. | Nicholas Culpeper, *Semeiotica Uranica*, 1651 (reported in Graeme Tobyn, 2022) | secondary synthesis.

IDENTIFIED_CONTRADICTIONS:
- **Functional Domain:** In the Western (Greco-Roman and English) traditions, Venus is associated with domesticity, love, and the "bed," and is explicitly contrasted with the violent nature of Mars. Conversely, the Mesoamerican tradition explicitly links Venus with "ritual war" and military cycles.
- **Iconography:** While the Western tradition personifies the planet as a beautiful female deity, the Maya Dresden Codex features a "Diving God" with a head represented by a Venus glyph, functioning as an agent of eclipses.

# MARS
DOMAIN:
- Mars illuminates the spheres of conflict, severity, and physical labor, acting as a "malefic" and "destructive" force characterized by excessive dryness. It governs the "fifth age of man" (later manhood, ages 41–55) and is utilized in medicine for its "cleansing and opening" qualities.
- **Is there consensus between ≥2 traditions with no known historical contact?** No; the loaded sources document a transmission of Mars's attributes from Mesopotamian foundations to Greco-Roman and subsequent Western European medical traditions, while noting that Mesoamerican architectural alignments for Mars are "much less certain" than those for the Sun, Moon, and Venus.

# JUPITER
DOMAIN:
- Jupiter illuminates the spheres of sovereign authority, biological growth, social justice, and prosperity; as a "benefic" and temperate force, it is associated with moderate warmth and moisture and governs the "sixth age of man" (full maturity, ages 56–67). Anatomically, it serves as the planetary ruler of the liver and lungs.
- **Is there consensus between ≥2 traditions with no known historical contact?** No; the loaded sources document a direct line of cultural and iconographic transmission from Mesopotamian planetary deities to the Greco-Roman and subsequent Western European medical traditions.

HISTORICAL_VOICES:
- **Mesopotamian** | 7th century BC | Recorded as one of the "errant" planetary gods in the *MUL.APIN* astronomical compendium. | Hunger & Pingree, *MUL.APIN: An Astronomical Compendium in Cuneiform*, 1989 | secondary synthesis.
- **Greco-Roman** | Classical Period | Known as *Phaethon* ("The Radiant/Shining One") and assigned to the god Zeus (Jupiter) as the sovereign of Olympus due to its majestic brightness and balanced transit. | "Celestial Symbols: A Critical Guide to Cosmographic Bibliography" | secondary synthesis.
- **Greco-Roman** | 2nd century AD | Defined as a "benefic" planet of a temperate nature (warming and moistening) that promotes growth and biological fertility. It governs the sixth age of man (full maturity, ages 56–67), characterized by retirement, independence, dignity, and deliberation. | Claudius Ptolemy, *Tetrabiblos* | primary source.
- **Greco-Roman** | 2nd century AD | Serves as the chief ruler of the triplicity comprising Aries, Leo, and Sagittarius, which governs the north-western quarter of the "inhabited world" (Europe), influencing nations like Spain toward independence and a love of cleanliness. | Claudius Ptolemy, *Tetrabiblos* | primary source.
- **English Star Medicine** | 17th century | Classified as a ruler of the liver and lungs and the signs Sagittarius and Pisces; practitioners utilized its associated herbs to treat ailments of these organs by sympathy. | Nicholas Culpeper, *The English Physitian Enlarged*, 1653 (reported in Graeme Tobyn, 2022) | secondary synthesis.
- **English Star Medicine** | 17th century | Identified as a "friend" to Venus but an "essential enemy" of Mercury, Mars, and Saturn. Its enmity with Mars is attributed to its love of "peace and justice" in contrast to the Martian love of "violence and oppressions." | Nicholas Culpeper, *Semeiotica Uranica*, 1651 (reported in Graeme Tobyn, 2022) | secondary synthesis.

IDENTIFIED_CONTRADICTIONS:
- **Inter-Planetary Relations:** While Ptolemy (2nd century AD) states that Mars "assists" Jupiter in the governance of the Aries triplicity, the later 17th-century tradition (Culpeper) identifies them as "essential enemies" due to their opposing moral conditions—peace versus violence.
- **Stellar Temperatures:** Ptolemy’s classification of fixed stars (reported in secondary synthesis) assigns the stars in the "claws" of the Scorpion (modern Libra) a temperature like that of Jupiter and Mercury, while modern analysis of Antares in the "body" identifies it as Mars-like.

*Note: There is a gap in the loaded sources regarding specific claims for Jupiter in Mesoamerican or Pre-Neolithic traditions, which are detailed for other celestial bodies; investigations into non-Western alignments for Jupiter are noted as "much less certain".*

# SATURN
DOMAIN:
Saturn illuminates the final stage of human life (old age) and the physical qualities of extreme cold and dryness, acting as a "malefic" or reductive force. It symbolizes the titan of time and agriculture and is utilized in medical practice for its astringent and binding operations.
**Is there consensus between ≥2 traditions with no known historical contact?** No; the loaded sources document a direct line of cultural and iconographic transmission from Mesopotamian planetary deities to the Greco-Roman and subsequent Western European medical traditions. While investigations into Mesoamerican architectural alignments have identified patterns for the Sun, Moon, and Venus, the relationship of alignments with Saturn is documented as "much less certain".

HISTORICAL_VOICES:
- **Mesopotamian** | 7th century BC | Identified as one of the "errant gods" whose movements were tracked for divination in the *MUL.APIN* astronomical compendium. | Hunger & Pingree, *MUL.APIN: An Astronomical Compendium in Cuneiform*, 1989 | secondary synthesis.
- **Greco-Roman** | Classical Period | Known as *Phainon* ("the imposing one") and assigned to Kronos (Saturn), the titan of time and agriculture, as the slowest and most imposing visible planet. | "Celestial Symbols: A Critical Guide to Cosmographic Bibliography" | secondary synthesis.
- **Greco-Roman** | 2nd century AD | Defined as a "malefic" planet of an excessively cooling and drying nature that governs the "seventh age of man" (old age, from 68 to death), characterized by weakness, decline, and dispiritedness. | Claudius Ptolemy, *Tetrabiblos* (as synthesized in "Tetrabiblos - Wikipedia") | primary source.
- **Greco-Roman** | 2nd century AD | Serves as the planetary domicile (house) of Capricorn and Aquarius and the anatomical significator of the spleen. | Claudius Ptolemy, *Tetrabiblos* (as synthesized in "Tetrabiblos - Wikipedia") | primary source.
- **Greco-Roman** | 2nd century AD | Fixed stars in the Hyades region are assigned a temperature "like that of Saturn and moderately like that of Mercury". | Claudius Ptolemy, *Tetrabiblos* (as synthesized in "Tetrabiblos - Wikipedia") | primary source.
- **English Star Medicine** | 17th century | Rules herbs with "cold and dry" operations (such as bistort) used to stop bleeding or fluxes, and is utilized antipathetically to treat ailments like throat infections. | Graeme Tobyn, *The English Physitian and the Star Medicine*, 2022 | secondary synthesis.
- **English Star Medicine** | 17th century | Classified as an "essential enemy" of the Sun, Moon, Jupiter, and Mars, but recognized as a friend to Mercury. | Nicholas Culpeper, *Semeiotica Uranica*, 1651 (reported in Graeme Tobyn, 2022) | secondary synthesis.

IDENTIFIED_CONTRADICTIONS:
None identified.

# URANUS
DOMAIN:
- Uranus illuminates the transition from organic mythological assimilation to institutional and nationalistic negotiation in celestial nomenclature; its discovery marks the beginning of the telescopic era that expanded the known solar system beyond the classical limit of Saturn.
- **Is there consensus between ≥2 traditions with no known historical contact?** No; the provided sources document Uranus's attributes and history solely within the context of modern Western scientific and institutional traditions following its discovery in 1781.

HISTORICAL_VOICES:
- **Modern European** | 1781 | Discovered accidentally by William Herschel, who attempted to name it *Georgium Sidus* ("The Star of George") to secure the patronage of King George III of England. | "Celestial Symbols: A Critical Guide to Cosmographic Bibliography" | secondary synthesis.
- **Modern European** | 18th–19th century | Continental astronomers (French, German, and American) rejected the British name, referring to the body as *Herschel* or proposing classical alternatives such as *Minerva* or *Oceanus*. | "Celestial Symbols: A Critical Guide to Cosmographic Bibliography" | secondary synthesis.
- **Modern European** | mid-19th century | The name *Uranus* (Ouranos) eventually prevailed based on Johann Elert Bode’s proposal to respect the classical theogony of succession: Jupiter (Zeus) was the son of Saturn (Kronos), and Saturn was the son of Uranus. | "Celestial Symbols: A Critical Guide to Cosmographic Bibliography" | secondary synthesis.
- **Western Astrology** | 2008–2009 | Uranus is integrated into the historical narrative of astrological development as a modern planetary body. | Nicholas Campion, *A History of Western Astrology* (cited in "Celestial Symbols") | secondary synthesis.

IDENTIFIED_CONTRADICTIONS:
- **Nomenclature:** A significant conflict existed between British astronomers and the international scientific community regarding the planet's name, pitting personal and nationalistic labels (*Georgium Sidus*, *Herschel*) against classical mythological proposals (*Uranus*, *Minerva*, *Oceanus*).

*Note: There is a total gap in the sources regarding Uranus in ancient Mesopotamian, Greco-Roman, or Mesoamerican traditions, as its discovery required telescopic instrumentation unavailable during those periods.*

# NEPTUNE
DOMAIN:
- Neptune illuminates the intersection of mathematical prediction and international geopolitical negotiation in celestial mapping; its naming process served to stabilize diplomatic tensions between imperial powers through the use of classical Roman oceanic mythology.
- **Is there consensus between ≥2 traditions with no known historical contact?** No; the loaded sources document Neptune’s history and attributes exclusively within the context of modern Western scientific and institutional traditions following its discovery in 1846.

HISTORICAL_VOICES:
- **Modern European** | 1846 | Localized by Johann Gottfried Galle based on the mathematical predictions of Urbain Le Verrier and John Couch Adams regarding gravitational perturbations in the orbit of Uranus. | "Celestial Symbols: A Critical Guide to Cosmographic Bibliography" | secondary synthesis.
- **Modern European** | 1846 | The name *Neptune* (Roman god of the oceans) was adopted internationally to neutralize rival personal and nationalistic proposals such as *Janus* (proposed by Galle in Germany) and *Oceanus* (defended in England). | "Celestial Symbols: A Critical Guide to Cosmographic Bibliography"; Nick Kollerstrom, *The Naming of Neptune*, 2009 | secondary synthesis.
- **Western Astrology** | 2008–2009 | Neptune is integrated into the historical survey of astrological development as a modern trans-Saturnian body. | Nicholas Campion, *A History of Western Astrology* (cited in "Celestial Symbols") | secondary synthesis.

IDENTIFIED_CONTRADICTIONS:
- **Nomenclature:** A documented conflict existed between German, British, and French scientific interests regarding the planet's name, involving competing proposals (*Janus*, *Oceanus*) before the international consolidation of *Neptune*.

*Note: There is a total gap in the sources regarding Neptune in ancient Mesopotamian, Greco-Roman, or Mesoamerican traditions, as its discovery required mathematical and telescopic capabilities unavailable during those periods.*

# PLUTO
DOMAIN:
- Pluto illuminates the intersection of classical mythological continuity and institutional memorialization within the modern era of celestial discovery; it represents the realm of the invisible, the underworld, and the "dark confines" beyond the reach of solar light.
- **Is there consensus between ≥2 traditions with no known historical contact?** No; the loaded sources document Pluto’s history and attributes exclusively within the context of 20th-century international scientific and institutional traditions following its discovery in 1930.

IDENTIFIED_CONTRADICTIONS:
